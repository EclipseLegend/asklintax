## Crypto taxes explained: when is crypto taxable?

**Foundation ID:** F-19 | **Category:** Investments & Crypto | **Difficulty:** Intermediate | **Tax Year:** 2025

**One-Sentence Answer:** The IRS treats cryptocurrency as property — every disposal (sale, trade, purchase, exchange) is a taxable event reported on Form 8949; holding crypto is not taxable, but trading one coin for another is.

**Action Required:** Pull complete transaction history from every exchange and wallet. Report all disposals on Form 8949 regardless of gain or loss. Answer the crypto question on Form 1040 honestly. Use crypto tax software (Koinly, TaxBit, CoinTracker) if you have activity across multiple platforms.

## YOUTUBE SCRIPT (6–7 min)

**Title:** Crypto Taxes Explained — What's Taxable and What's Not (2025 Guide)

**[HOOK]** If you bought, sold, or traded cryptocurrency this year, you have tax reporting obligations — even if you lost money. The IRS has been clear: crypto is property, and every disposal is a taxable event. Today I'll walk through exactly what's taxable, what's not, and how to report it.

**[PROPERTY NOT CURRENCY]** The foundational IRS rule: cryptocurrency is classified as property, not foreign currency. This is important because it means: every time you dispose of crypto — sell it, trade it, buy something with it — you have a potential capital gain or loss. There's no de minimis exception. Every event, regardless of size, must be reported.

**[TAXABLE EVENTS]**
Taxable: Selling crypto for USD, trading one crypto for another, buying goods or services with crypto, receiving staking rewards or mining income, receiving airdrops, being paid in crypto for work.

NOT taxable: Simply holding crypto (even with gains), transferring between your own wallets, buying crypto with USD (this establishes cost basis, not a taxable event), gifting crypto under the annual gift limit.

The one that surprises most people: trading Bitcoin for Ethereum is a taxable sale of Bitcoin at fair market value at the time of the trade. Every crypto-to-crypto swap is a taxable event.

**[SHORT VS LONG TERM]**
Held 1 year or less → short-term capital gains → taxed at your ordinary income rate (10–37%).
Held more than 1 year → long-term capital gains → taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income. Most taxpayers pay 15%.

The incentive to hold is significant. Short-term rates can be twice as high as long-term rates.

**[CALCULATING GAIN/LOSS]** Proceeds minus cost basis equals your gain or loss.
Example: Bought 1 ETH for $2,000. Sold 14 months later for $3,500. Gain: $1,500. Long-term rate applies. Keep the date of purchase and purchase price (in USD) for every acquisition — that's your cost basis.

**[LOSSES]** Capital losses offset capital gains. If your losses exceed gains, you can deduct up to $3,000 against ordinary income per year. Excess losses carry forward. A bad crypto year can actually reduce your overall tax bill.

**[THE 1040 QUESTION]** Every Form 1040 asks whether you received, sold, exchanged, or disposed of any digital asset. Answer this honestly — even if your answer is no. Answering no falsely when you had crypto activity is a misrepresentation on a federal tax return.

**[HOW TO REPORT]** Form 8949 for every disposal. Totals flow to Schedule D. Most tax software imports directly from major exchanges. For multiple wallets or DeFi activity, use dedicated crypto tax software — Koinly, TaxBit, or CoinTracker.
