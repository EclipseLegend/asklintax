# Foundation 14 — Content Suite
## What can I deduct as a small business owner?

---

## KNOWLEDGE OBJECT SUMMARY

**Foundation ID:** F-14
**Category:** Small Business & Self-Employment
**User Emotion:** Organizing (📋)
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**CPA Reviewed:** Yes
**Tax Year:** 2025

**One-Sentence Answer:**
Any expense that is "ordinary and necessary" for your business is deductible on Schedule C — reducing both your income tax and your self-employment tax simultaneously, making deductions worth 27–38 cents per dollar depending on your tax bracket.

**Action Required:**
Open a dedicated business bank account and credit card immediately. Start tracking all business expenses now — track first, decide eligibility later. Every $1,000 in deductions saves approximately $275–$382 in combined taxes.

**[HOOK — 0:00 to 0:30]**

One of the biggest financial advantages of being self-employed is the ability to deduct business expenses — reducing both your income tax and your self-employment tax at the same time.

But a lot of small business owners either miss deductions they're entitled to, or claim things they shouldn't. Today I'm going to walk through the major deduction categories, explain what qualifies, and tell you what the IRS specifically says you cannot deduct.

---

**[THE GOLDEN RULE — 0:30 to 1:15]**

Every business deduction must pass the same test: was it ordinary and necessary for your business?

Ordinary means it's common in your industry. A web designer buying software — ordinary. That same designer buying a sports car — not ordinary.

Necessary means it's helpful and appropriate for your business — not that it's required. Just relevant and reasonable.

If you can say yes to both, it's likely deductible. When in doubt, keep the receipt. You can always decide later whether it qualifies. You can't reconstruct records you never kept.

---

**[WHY DEDUCTIONS MATTER MORE FOR SELF-EMPLOYED — 1:15 to 2:00]**

Here's something W-2 employees don't have: when you're self-employed, your business deductions reduce your Schedule C net profit. And that net profit is subject to BOTH income tax and self-employment tax — 15.3%.

So when you deduct $1,000 as a business expense, you're not just saving your income tax rate on it. You're also saving 15.3% in self-employment tax. In the 22% bracket, a $1,000 deduction saves you roughly $363.

That's why tracking expenses matters so much more when you're self-employed.

---

**[THE BIG CATEGORIES — 2:00 to 5:30]**

Let me go through the major categories.

Home office. If you have a dedicated space used regularly and exclusively for business, you can deduct it. The simplified method: $5 per square foot, up to 300 square feet, so maximum $1,500 per year. The regular method uses the actual percentage of your home — more paperwork, potentially higher deduction. Key word: exclusively. A desk in your bedroom you also use personally doesn't qualify.

Vehicle. Two methods. Standard mileage rate: 70 cents per mile for 2025. Keep a mileage log. Actual expenses: track all car costs and multiply by business use percentage. Commuting to your regular office doesn't count. Client visits, business errands, picking up supplies — those do.

Equipment and technology. Computers, phones, software subscriptions — deductible based on business use percentage. Software subscriptions are often 100% business: Adobe, QuickBooks, Zoom, GitHub. You may be able to deduct the full cost of equipment in the year you buy it under Section 179 or bonus depreciation.

Professional services. CPA fees, legal fees for business matters, business consultants, professional memberships — all deductible.

Marketing. Google ads, Facebook ads, website hosting, business cards, domain registration — fully deductible.

Meals. Business meals with clients, employees, or business partners are 50% deductible. You must have a clear business purpose. Keep the receipt and note who was there and what you discussed. Solo meals while working are NOT deductible unless you're traveling overnight for business. Entertainment — concerts, sporting events, golf — is no longer deductible under current law.

Self-employed health insurance. If you're self-employed and not covered by an employer plan, you can deduct 100% of your health insurance premiums as an above-the-line deduction. This reduces your AGI, not just your taxable income — it's especially valuable.

Retirement contributions. SEP-IRA, Solo 401(k), SIMPLE IRA contributions are deductible. The SEP-IRA allows contributions up to 25% of net self-employment income — a significant tax reduction tool for profitable businesses.

---

**[WHAT YOU CANNOT DEDUCT — 5:30 to 6:30]**

Equal time for what you can't deduct:

Personal expenses — anything not related to business.
Commuting — the drive from home to your regular office is not deductible.
Fines and penalties — IRS penalties, parking tickets.
Clothing — unless it's a required uniform you can't wear outside of work.
Entertainment — concerts, sporting events, golf — no longer deductible.
Solo meals at your desk — unless traveling for business.

---

**[RECORD KEEPING — 6:30 to 7:15]**

The minimum you need for each expense: a receipt and a note about the business purpose. For meals, add who was there and what was discussed.

The simplest system: use a dedicated business bank account and business credit card for everything. Every transaction is automatically recorded. At year end, export and categorize. Much faster than sorting through personal accounts.

**Small Business Deductions (Schedule C, Tax Year 2025):**

**Standard:** All deductions must be "ordinary and necessary" for the business. Ordinary = common in the industry. Necessary = helpful and appropriate (not required).

**Value of deductions for self-employed:** Reduces net profit subject to BOTH income tax AND self-employment tax (15.3%). $1,000 deduction saves ~$275–$382 depending on bracket.

**Major deduction categories:**
- Home office: $5/sq ft simplified method (max $1,500) or actual % of home costs. Must be used REGULARLY and EXCLUSIVELY for business.
- Vehicle: Standard mileage 70¢/mile (2025) with mileage log, or actual expenses × business %.
- Equipment/tech: Business % of computers, phones, software subscriptions.
- Professional services: CPA, legal (business only), consultants, memberships.
- Marketing: All advertising, website costs, business cards — 100% deductible.
- Meals: 50% deductible with clear business purpose; solo meals not deductible.
- Entertainment: NOT deductible (eliminated post-2017 TCJA).
- Self-employed health insurance: 100% above-the-line deduction (reduces AGI).
- Retirement contributions: SEP-IRA up to 25% of net SE income.

**Not deductible:** Personal expenses, commuting, fines/penalties, regular clothing, entertainment, solo meals (unless traveling), personal portion of mixed expenses.

**Records required:** Receipt + business purpose note. For meals: who attended + business discussed. For vehicle: mileage log with date/destination/purpose.

**Best practice:** Dedicated business bank account + business credit card = automatic record keeping.
