# Foundation 20 — Content Suite
## FBAR: do I need to report my foreign bank accounts?

**Foundation ID:** F-20 | **Category:** Investments & Foreign Accounts | **Difficulty:** Intermediate | **Tax Year:** 2025

**One-Sentence Answer:** If you're a U.S. person (citizen, green card holder, or resident alien) whose foreign financial accounts exceeded $10,000 combined at any point during the year, you must file FinCEN Form 114 (FBAR) by April 15 at bsaefiling.fincen.treas.gov — separate from your tax return.

**Action Required:** Add up the maximum balances of all your foreign accounts during the year. If the combined total ever exceeded $10,000, file FBAR at bsaefiling.fincen.treas.gov by April 15 (auto-extended to Oct 15). This is separate from your IRS tax return.

## YOUTUBE SCRIPT (6–7 min)

**Title:** FBAR Explained — Do You Need to Report Your Taiwan or China Bank Accounts?

**[HOOK]** If you moved to the United States from Taiwan or China and you still have bank accounts back home — you may have a filing obligation most people have never heard of. It's called FBAR. Missing it can result in penalties of up to $10,000 per account per year. Today I'll explain exactly what it is, who must file, and how to do it.

**[WHAT IS FBAR]** FBAR stands for Foreign Bank Account Report — officially FinCEN Form 114. It's a disclosure requirement under the Bank Secrecy Act. It is not a tax. You pay nothing when you file it. You're simply telling the U.S. government that you have financial accounts outside the United States.

**[THE THRESHOLD]** You must file FBAR if you're a U.S. person and your foreign financial accounts combined exceeded $10,000 at any single point during the calendar year. Not the year-end balance. Not the average balance. Any single point during the year.

This is a combined threshold. Two accounts, each at $6,000 — combined maximum $12,000 — triggers FBAR for both accounts.

**[WHO MUST FILE]** U.S. persons: citizens, green card holders, resident aliens (those who pass the Substantial Presence Test). This includes most H-1B holders who have been in the U.S. long enough.

F-1 students in their first 5 years are generally not U.S. persons for FBAR purposes. J-1 visitors in their first 2 years are also generally exempt.

**[WHAT ACCOUNTS]** Bank accounts (savings, checking, fixed deposits), brokerage accounts, mutual funds, pension accounts, life insurance with cash value, and accounts where you have signature authority — even if the money isn't yours.

If your parents in Taiwan put your name on their account as a convenience signer, that account may count toward your FBAR threshold.

**[HOW TO FILE]** File electronically at bsaefiling.fincen.treas.gov. This is NOT IRS.gov. FBAR is filed with FinCEN, not the IRS. The deadline is April 15 with an automatic extension to October 15 — no need to request the extension.

For each account, you report: bank name and address, account number, account type, and maximum value during the year converted to USD.

**[FBAR vs FATCA]** Many people with foreign accounts must file both FBAR and FATCA (Form 8938). Different thresholds, different agencies, not interchangeable. FBAR: $10,000 combined, filed with FinCEN. FATCA: $50,000 single / $100,000 MFJ at year-end, filed with IRS on your tax return.

**[MISSED FILINGS]** If you have unfiled FBARs from prior years, don't ignore it. The IRS Streamlined Filing Procedures allow non-willful late filers to come forward with significantly reduced penalties. Voluntarily disclosing is always better than being discovered.

**FBAR (FinCEN Form 114) — Foreign Bank Account Report:**

**Who must file:** U.S. persons (citizens, green card holders, resident aliens/SPT passers) with combined foreign financial account balances exceeding $10,000 at any single point during the calendar year.

**NOT required for:** F-1 students (first 5 years), J-1 visitors (first 2 years), nonresident aliens generally.

**$10,000 threshold:** Combined across ALL foreign accounts at any single point in the year. Not year-end, not average. If combined max exceeded $10,000 at any moment → file, reporting ALL foreign accounts.

**What to report:** Bank accounts, brokerage accounts, mutual funds, pension accounts, life insurance with cash value, accounts with signature authority (even if not your money).

**NOT reported:** Real estate held directly, precious metals held personally, foreign currency not in accounts, social insurance programs.

**How to file:** Electronically at bsaefiling.fincen.treas.gov (NOT IRS.gov). Free.

**Deadline:** April 15, automatic extension to October 15 (no request needed).

**FBAR vs FATCA (Form 8938):**
- FBAR: FinCEN, $10,000 threshold, separate from tax return
- FATCA: IRS, $50,000 single/$100,000 MFJ threshold, filed with tax return
- Both may apply simultaneously — filing one does not satisfy the other

**Penalties:** Non-willful: up to $10,000/violation/year. Willful: up to greater of $100,000 or 50% of account balance/violation/year.

**Late filers:** Streamlined Filing Procedures available for non-willful violations — consult CPA/attorney.
