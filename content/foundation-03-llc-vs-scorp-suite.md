## LLC vs S-Corp: which is right for your business?

---

**User Emotion:** Deciding (🤔)

**One-Sentence Answer:**
An LLC and S-Corp are not opposites — most profitable small businesses have an LLC that elects to be taxed as an S-Corp, saving self-employment taxes on the portion of income taken as distributions rather than salary.

**[HOOK — 0:00 to 0:30]**

"Should I have an LLC or an S-Corp?" This is one of the most common questions I get from Chinese small business owners — and also one of the most misunderstood.

Here's the thing: LLC and S-Corp are not two different options where you pick one. They operate on completely different levels. And once you understand that, the whole decision becomes much clearer.

Today I'm going to explain exactly what each one is, show you real numbers, and give you a simple income threshold to know which situation makes sense for you.

---

**[THE KEY INSIGHT — 0:30 to 2:00]**

First, the most important thing to understand.

LLC is a legal structure. It's how your business is registered with your state.

S-Corp is a tax election. It's how the IRS decides to tax your business income.

They're not the same category of thing. Which means they're not mutually exclusive.

In fact, the most common setup for profitable small businesses is: an LLC that elects to be taxed as an S-Corp. You get the legal simplicity of an LLC, combined with the tax benefits of S-Corp treatment.

So when people ask "LLC or S-Corp," they're really asking: "should my LLC elect S-Corp tax treatment?" The LLC itself stays exactly the same — only the tax treatment changes.

---

**[HOW EACH IS TAXED — 2:00 to 4:00]**

Let me explain the tax difference with real numbers.

By default, if you have an LLC and you're the only owner, the IRS treats all your business profit as personal income. Let's say you net $100,000 this year. You pay self-employment tax — that's 15.3% for Social Security and Medicare — on the entire $100,000. That's about $14,130 in self-employment tax alone. Plus federal income tax on top.

Now, with an S-Corp election, your income gets split into two buckets.

Bucket one: your salary. You pay yourself, say, $60,000 as an employee. You pay payroll taxes on that — same rate as self-employment tax.

Bucket two: distributions. The remaining $40,000 passes through to you as owner distributions. And here's the key — distributions are NOT subject to self-employment tax.

So instead of paying 15.3% on $100,000, you're only paying it on $60,000. That's roughly $5,600 in savings.

Minus the additional costs of running an S-Corp — payroll service, CPA fees for the business return — you might net $2,000 to $4,000 in real savings per year. At $100,000 in profit.

At higher income levels, the savings grow significantly.

---

**[THE INCOME THRESHOLD — 4:00 to 5:30]**

So at what income does an S-Corp start making sense?

Here's the general guideline most CPAs use:

Under $40,000 in net profit — not worth it. The overhead costs of running an S-Corp will likely eat up any savings.

Between $40,000 and $60,000 — maybe. Run the numbers with a CPA. It depends on your state and what accounting costs you're already paying.

Over $60,000 — usually worth it.

Over $100,000 — strongly worth considering.

The crossover point is roughly $50,000 in annual net profit for most situations. Below that, stay with default LLC taxation. Above that, it's worth a serious conversation with a CPA.

---

**[THE REAL COSTS — 5:30 to 7:00]**

Now, I want to be honest about the additional requirements, because the savings are real but so is the work.

With an S-Corp, you need to file a separate business tax return — Form 1120-S — due March 15. More complex than a Schedule C, almost always needs a CPA.

You need to run payroll for yourself as an employee, file quarterly payroll tax returns, and issue yourself a W-2 at year end. You'll want a payroll service like Gusto or QuickBooks Payroll.

And the IRS has a requirement called "reasonable salary" — you can't just pay yourself $1 to maximize your distributions. Your salary has to be comparable to what you'd pay someone else to do your job. Getting this wrong can trigger an audit.

Factor in $1,500 to $3,000 per year in additional accounting and payroll costs before deciding if the S-Corp saves you enough to be worth it.

---

**[KEY RESTRICTION FOR IMMIGRANTS — 7:00 to 7:30]**

One thing specifically important for Chinese families.

S-Corp shares can only be owned by U.S. citizens and permanent residents — green card holders.

If you're on a visa — H1-B, L1, F1 OPT, or any non-immigrant status — you're currently ineligible to own S-Corp shares.

This changes if you get your green card. But until then, the S-Corp election isn't available to you, and your LLC will remain taxed as a default sole proprietor.

---

**[OUTRO — 7:30 to 8:00]**

The bottom line: LLC and S-Corp aren't opposites. They work together. If your LLC is consistently making more than $50,000 per year, it's worth a conversation with a CPA about the S-Corp election.

## AI KNOWLEDGE SNIPPET

LLC (legal structure) and S-Corp (tax election) are not mutually exclusive. An LLC can elect S-Corp taxation by filing Form 2553 with the IRS.

Default LLC taxation: all net profit subject to 15.3% self-employment tax on owner's personal return (Schedule C).

S-Corp taxation: income split between owner salary (subject to payroll taxes) and owner distributions (NOT subject to self-employment/payroll taxes). Tax savings come from the distribution portion.

Income threshold guideline:
- Under $40K net profit: S-Corp overhead likely exceeds savings
- $40K–$60K: case-by-case, consult CPA
- Over $60K: usually worth electing S-Corp status

Additional S-Corp requirements: separate Form 1120-S return (due March 15), mandatory owner payroll with quarterly filings, "reasonable salary" requirement enforced by IRS.

Eligibility restriction: S-Corp shares can only be owned by U.S. citizens and permanent residents (not visa holders).
