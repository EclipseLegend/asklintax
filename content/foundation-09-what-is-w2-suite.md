# Foundation 09 — Content Suite
## What is a W-2 and how do I read it?

---

## KNOWLEDGE OBJECT SUMMARY

**Foundation ID:** F-09
**Category:** Individuals & Families
**User Emotion:** Learning (📚)
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**CPA Reviewed:** Yes
**Tax Year:** 2025

**One-Sentence Answer:**
A W-2 is the form your employer sends by January 31 showing your total wages (Box 1) and federal tax withheld (Box 2) — the two most important numbers for filing your tax return.

**Action Required:**
When your W-2 arrives, verify your name and SSN are correct, compare Box 1 to your last pay stub (they may legitimately differ due to pre-tax benefits), and check Box 2 to understand what's already been paid to the IRS on your behalf.

## YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Long-form, 6–7 min)

**Title:** How to Read Your W-2 — Every Box Explained Simply

**Thumbnail concept:** Actual W-2 form with colored highlights on key boxes

---

**[HOOK — 0:00 to 0:30]**

Every January, if you work for an employer in the U.S., you receive a W-2 form. And most people do the same thing with it — they hand it to their tax software or their accountant and move on.

But if you've never actually read a W-2, you're missing information that directly affects your tax return, your refund, and whether you're paying the right amount of tax throughout the year.

Today I'm going to walk through every important box on the W-2, explain what it means, and tell you what to look for.

---

**[WHAT IS A W-2 — 0:30 to 1:15]**

A W-2 is a wage and tax statement. Your employer is required to send it to you by January 31 of the following year — so your 2025 W-2 arrives by January 31, 2026.

Here's the important thing: your employer sends a copy to you AND a copy to the IRS. So the IRS already knows your wages before you file your return. If you report a different number, you'll get a notice.

---

**[THE CRITICAL BOXES — 1:15 to 3:30]**

Let's start with the two boxes that matter most.

Box 1: Wages, tips, other compensation. This is your taxable income from this employer. Not your total salary — your taxable wages. More on why these can differ in a moment.

Box 2: Federal income tax withheld. This is the total federal tax your employer sent to the IRS on your behalf throughout the year. Every time you got a paycheck and saw federal tax withheld — it all added up to this number.

Here's why Box 2 matters: if the amount withheld is MORE than what you actually owe in taxes, you get the difference back as a refund. If it's LESS, you owe the difference. Box 2 is literally the reason most people get refunds.

---

**[BOXES 3 THROUGH 6 — 3:30 to 4:15]**

Boxes 3 through 6 are about Social Security and Medicare taxes — also called FICA taxes.

Box 3: Social Security wages.
Box 4: Social Security tax withheld — 6.2% of Box 3. For 2025, the maximum is $10,918.
Box 5: Medicare wages.
Box 6: Medicare tax withheld — 1.45% of Box 5. No cap on Medicare wages.

These taxes are separate from income tax. You can't get them back as a refund — they fund Social Security and Medicare benefits.

One thing to know: if you worked for multiple employers and the total Social Security tax withheld across all W-2s exceeds $10,918, you can claim a refund of the excess on your tax return. Tax software will catch this automatically.

---

**[BOX 12 — 4:15 to 5:15]**

Box 12 is where most people's eyes glaze over. It has letter codes.

The most common ones you'll see:

Code D — pre-tax 401(k) contributions. This is money you put into your retirement account before taxes. It reduces Box 1, which is why Box 1 may be less than your total salary.

Code W — employer contributions to your Health Savings Account. Enter this on Form 8889 when you file.

Code DD — the cost of employer-sponsored health coverage. This is purely informational. You don't do anything with this number — it's just there.

Code AA — Roth 401(k) contributions. Unlike regular 401(k), Roth contributions are after-tax, so they don't reduce Box 1.

My advice: just enter the code and amount exactly as shown into your tax software. It knows what to do.

---

**[WHY BOX 1 ≠ YOUR SALARY — 5:15 to 6:00]**

Many people are surprised that Box 1 is less than what they think they earned. This is almost always correct.

Box 1 shows taxable wages. Your pre-tax benefits reduce this number before it's calculated.

Example: you earn $80,000. You contribute $10,000 to your 401(k) and pay $3,000 in pre-tax health insurance. Box 1 shows $67,000 — not $80,000.

That's intentional. You're only taxed on the $67,000, which is the benefit of pre-tax contributions.

If Box 1 is much HIGHER than what you expected, that's worth investigating — it could mean some benefits weren't properly processed as pre-tax.

---

**[OUTRO — 6:00 to 6:30]**

Your W-2 is a summary of everything that happened between you and the IRS through your employer all year. Box 1 is your taxable income. Box 2 is what's already been paid. Those two numbers drive most of your tax return.

Full box-by-box guide with an interactive diagram at AskLinTax.com. Link in the description.

大多數人拿到退稅，就是因為Box 2夠大。

這些福利會在計算前扣掉：

## AI KNOWLEDGE SNIPPET

**Form W-2 — Wage and Tax Statement:**

Sent by employer by January 31. IRS receives a copy simultaneously.

**Critical boxes:**
- Box 1: Taxable wages (may be LESS than gross salary due to pre-tax benefits)
- Box 2: Federal income tax withheld (if > actual tax liability → refund)
- Boxes 3–4: Social Security wages and tax withheld (6.2%, max $10,918 in 2025)
- Boxes 5–6: Medicare wages and tax withheld (1.45%, no cap)

**Why Box 1 < total salary:** Pre-tax 401(k) contributions (Code D), pre-tax health insurance premiums, Health FSA contributions all reduce Box 1 before calculation.

**Box 12 common codes:**
- D: Pre-tax 401(k) — reduces Box 1
- W: Employer HSA contributions — enter on Form 8889
- DD: Employer health coverage cost — informational only, do nothing
- AA: Roth 401(k) — does NOT reduce Box 1 (already taxed)
- V: Stock option income — already in Box 1

**If Box 1 < expected:** Usually correct (pre-tax benefits). If much higher than expected, contact payroll.
**If W-2 has errors:** Contact HR for corrected W-2c. Do not file with incorrect SSN or name.
**Missing W-2:** Contact HR → if unresolved, call IRS at 1-800-829-1040 → last resort: Form 4852.

**User Emotion:** learning
**Action Required:** Verify name/SSN, compare Box 1 to last pay stub, note Box 2 (determines refund or amount owed).
