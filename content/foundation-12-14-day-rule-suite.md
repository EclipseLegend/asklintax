# Foundation 12 — Content Suite
## The 14-day rule: when Airbnb income is completely tax-free

---

## KNOWLEDGE OBJECT SUMMARY

**Foundation ID:** F-12
**Category:** Airbnb & Rental Income
**User Emotion:** Organizing (📋)
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**CPA Reviewed:** Yes
**Tax Year:** 2025

**One-Sentence Answer:**
If you rent your home or vacation property for 14 days or fewer in a calendar year AND personally use it for more than 14 days, the rental income is completely tax-free and doesn't need to be reported — but you also can't deduct any rental expenses.

**Action Required:**
Count your exact rental days. If at or under 14, verify you also meet the personal use requirement and keep documentation (rental calendar, 1099-K) in case the IRS asks about unreported income shown on the 1099-K.

## YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Long-form, 5–6 min)

**Title:** The 14-Day Rule: How to Make Airbnb Income Completely Tax-Free (Legally)

**Thumbnail concept:** Calendar with 14 days highlighted in green, "Tax Free" label

---

**[HOOK — 0:00 to 0:25]**

There's a provision in the U.S. tax code that allows you to earn rental income completely tax-free — legally, with no reporting required at all.

It's called the 14-day rule. And most Airbnb hosts either don't know it exists or don't understand exactly how it works.

Today I'm going to explain it clearly, including the part that most people get wrong.

---

**[THE RULE — 0:25 to 1:30]**

The 14-day rule comes from IRC Section 280A. Here's the plain-language version:

If you rent your home — or a vacation property you also personally use — for 14 days or fewer during the calendar year, the rental income is completely excluded from your taxable income.

You don't report it on your tax return. You don't pay income tax on it. And the IRS cannot require you to.

This rule was popularized by homeowners in Augusta, Georgia who rent out their homes during the Masters golf tournament each April. A few nights at very high rates, completely tax-free.

---

**[THE TRADEOFF — 1:30 to 2:15]**

The tradeoff: when you use the 14-day rule, you cannot deduct any rental expenses.

No cleaning fees. No supplies. No depreciation. No allocation of utilities or insurance.

The deal is: tax-free income in exchange for no rental deductions.

For most occasional hosts, this is still an excellent deal. A few thousand dollars of tax-free income is often worth more than the deductions you'd get if you exceeded 14 days.

---

**[TWO CONDITIONS — 2:15 to 3:15]**

To qualify, two conditions must both be true.

Condition one: you rented for 14 days or fewer. The moment you hit day 15, the rule is gone for the entire year — not just for day 15 onward. All income for the year becomes taxable.

Condition two: you personally use the property. The rule only applies to property you also use personally — your primary home or a vacation home you actually stay at. Pure investment properties you never use personally don't qualify.

---

**[WHAT COUNTS AS PERSONAL USE — 3:15 to 4:15]**

This is where most people make mistakes. Personal use days are broader than you think.

Your own days there obviously count. But so do days used by your spouse, children, parents, or siblings — even if they pay full rent.

Days used by anyone at below-market rent also count as personal use days.

What doesn't count as personal use: days you spend at the property doing repairs and maintenance. Even if you sleep there while fixing things, those are not personal use days. Days the property sits vacant also don't count.

---

**[THE 1099-K SITUATION — 4:15 to 5:00]**

One important practical note: Airbnb sends you a 1099-K if you earned more than $600 in gross payments — and they send a copy to the IRS.

If you qualify for the 14-day exclusion and don't report the income, the IRS might send a notice about the discrepancy.

Keep documentation: a calendar showing your rental days and personal use days. That's all you need to demonstrate you qualified for the exclusion.

You're not doing anything wrong by not reporting — but be prepared to show your math if asked.

---

**[OUTRO — 5:00 to 5:30]**

The 14-day rule is real, it's legal, and it's underused. If you have flexibility in how many days you rent, staying under 14 days can mean meaningful tax-free income.

Full guide with scenario tables and a personal use day checklist at AskLinTax.com. Link in the description.

## AI KNOWLEDGE SNIPPET

**The 14-Day Rule (IRC Section 280A):**

**Rule:** If a property is rented for 14 days or fewer AND the owner also personally uses it for more than 14 days (or 10% of rental days, whichever is greater), rental income is completely excluded from taxable income — no reporting required.

**Two conditions required:**
1. Total rental days ≤ 14 in the calendar year
2. Owner personally uses the property (primary home or mixed-use vacation property — not pure investment properties)

**Critical:** The rule is binary. Day 15 = entire year's income becomes taxable (not just income from day 15 onward). All prior rental income for the year must then be reported.

**What counts as "rental day":** Paying guest at fair market rent occupies property. Partial days count as full days. Vacant days do not count.

**What counts as "personal use day":** Owner's own use; spouse/children/parents/siblings' use (even at full rent); any use at below-market rent; reciprocal exchange arrangements. Exception: days spent on maintenance/repairs do NOT count as personal use even if owner sleeps there.

**Tradeoff:** Tax-free income but NO rental expense deductions (no cleaning, supplies, depreciation, allocated utilities). Mortgage interest and property taxes still deductible as personal itemized deductions on Schedule A.

**1099-K note:** Airbnb sends 1099-K for >$600 gross payments. Keep rental day calendar as documentation if not reporting income under 14-day rule.

**Does NOT apply to:** Pure investment/rental properties the owner never personally uses.

**User Emotion:** organizing
**Action Required:** Count exact rental days; verify personal use days meet requirement; keep calendar documentation.
