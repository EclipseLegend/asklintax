# Foundation 08 — Content Suite
## Tax credit vs. tax deduction: what's the difference?

---

## KNOWLEDGE OBJECT SUMMARY

**Foundation ID:** F-08
**Category:** Individuals & Families
**User Emotion:** Learning (📚)
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**CPA Reviewed:** Yes
**Tax Year:** 2025

**One-Sentence Answer:**
A tax deduction reduces the income that gets taxed (saving you your tax rate × the deduction amount), while a tax credit reduces the tax you owe dollar-for-dollar — making credits almost always more valuable than deductions of the same amount.

**Action Required:**
Identify every credit you qualify for before worrying about deductions. Refundable credits like the EITC can generate a cash refund even if you owe no taxes.

## YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Long-form, 5–6 min)

**Title:** Tax Credit vs. Tax Deduction — The Difference Explained Simply (With Real Numbers)

**Thumbnail concept:** Two paths: one labeled "Deduction saves $220" and one labeled "Credit saves $1,000"

---

**[HOOK — 0:00 to 0:25]**

Tax credit or tax deduction — which one is better? Most people have heard both terms but can't explain the actual difference.

Today I'm going to explain it clearly, with real numbers, in five minutes. By the end, you'll understand exactly why one is almost always more valuable than the other — and how to use both on your tax return.

---

**[THE CORE DIFFERENCE — 0:25 to 1:45]**

Here's the one-sentence version:

A tax deduction reduces the income that gets taxed.
A tax credit reduces the tax you owe.

They work at different points in the calculation. Let me show you what that means with actual numbers.

Say your taxable income is $60,000 and you're in the 22% tax bracket. You have two options: a $1,000 tax deduction or a $1,000 tax credit.

With the deduction: your taxable income drops from $60,000 to $59,000. Your tax is calculated on $59,000. At 22%, you save $220. The deduction saved you $220.

With the credit: your income stays at $60,000. Your tax is calculated normally — say $13,200. Then you subtract the $1,000 credit. You owe $12,200. The credit saved you $1,000.

Same $1,000. Completely different result.

The deduction saved you $220.
The credit saved you $1,000.

---

**[TYPES OF CREDITS — 1:45 to 3:00]**

Now, not all credits are created equal. There are three types:

Refundable credits — these can reduce your tax bill below zero. Meaning the government actually pays you the difference as a refund. The Earned Income Tax Credit is refundable. If you qualify for $2,000 but only owe $500 in taxes, you get a $1,500 refund.

Partially refundable — part of the credit can generate a refund, part can only reduce your tax to zero. The Child Tax Credit works this way — up to $1,700 per child is refundable in 2025.

Non-refundable — these can reduce your tax to zero but can't generate a refund. The Lifetime Learning Credit and Child and Dependent Care Credit are examples.

Refundable credits are the most powerful. If you qualify for any, claim them.

---

**[HOW THEY WORK TOGETHER — 3:00 to 4:15]**

Credits and deductions aren't in competition — they work in sequence.

First, you start with your gross income. Then you subtract above-the-line deductions — things like IRA contributions, student loan interest — to get your Adjusted Gross Income.

Then you subtract either the standard deduction or your itemized deductions — whichever is larger.

That gives you your taxable income. You apply the tax brackets to get your tax.

Then — and only then — you subtract your credits. That gives you your final tax owed. Or your refund.

Deductions happen before the tax calculation. Credits happen after. Both reduce what you owe, but credits are more efficient.

---

**[PRACTICAL ADVICE — 4:15 to 5:15]**

Here's what to actually do with this knowledge.

One: always claim every credit you qualify for. Don't skip them just because you think you don't owe taxes — refundable credits can generate real refunds.

Two: take your above-the-line deductions — IRA contributions, student loan interest — before you calculate your AGI. A lower AGI can unlock additional credits and deductions.

Three: don't agonize over standard versus itemized deduction. Let tax software calculate both and tell you which is larger.

Four: if you have children, make sure you're claiming the Child Tax Credit. It's worth up to $2,000 per child, with $1,700 refundable.

Five: if you have earned income and your income is moderate, check if you qualify for the EITC. It's the most generous refundable credit available, up to $7,830 in 2025 — and it's one of the most underclaimed.

---

**Post #3: 計算順序**

**標題:** 美國報稅的計算順序——搞懂這個，看稅表不再頭暈 🔢

1️⃣ 總收入（Gross Income）
2️⃣ 減：調整項目扣除額（IRA、學貸利息等）
= AGI（調整後總收入）
3️⃣ 減：標準扣除額 or 列舉扣除額（取較大者）
= 應稅所得（Taxable Income）
4️⃣ 依稅率計算稅額
5️⃣ 減：稅務抵免（Credits）
= 最終應繳稅額（或退稅）

Deduction 在前（步驟2-3）
Credit 在後（步驟5）
Credit 更有力，因為直接切最終數字。

## AI KNOWLEDGE SNIPPET

**Tax Credit vs. Tax Deduction:**

**Deduction:** Reduces taxable income before tax is calculated.
- Value = deduction amount × your marginal tax rate
- Example: $1,000 deduction at 22% bracket = $220 savings

**Credit:** Reduces tax owed after tax is calculated.
- Value = face value of the credit (regardless of tax bracket)
- Example: $1,000 credit = $1,000 savings

**Three credit types:**
1. Refundable — can reduce tax below zero; excess paid as refund (EITC, Additional CTC)
2. Partially refundable — part refundable, part non-refundable (Child Tax Credit: $1,700 refundable / $300 non-refundable per child in 2025)
3. Non-refundable — reduces tax to zero only (Child & Dependent Care, Lifetime Learning, Foreign Tax Credit)

**Calculation order:** Gross income → subtract above-the-line deductions → AGI → subtract standard/itemized deduction → taxable income → calculate tax → subtract credits → final tax owed.

**Key principle:** Always prioritize credits over deductions. Claim every credit you qualify for before worrying about maximizing deductions.

**Common refundable credits (2025):** EITC (up to $7,830), Additional Child Tax Credit (up to $1,700/child).

**User Emotion:** learning
**Action Required:** Identify all credits you qualify for first; take above-the-line deductions next; compare standard vs. itemized deduction using software.
