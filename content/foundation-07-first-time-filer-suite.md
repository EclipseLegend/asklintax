**One-Sentence Answer:**
Filing your first U.S. tax return takes five steps: gather documents (especially your W-2), choose your filing status, pick a filing method (software or CPA), complete and submit the return by April 15, then save a copy and track your refund.

**Action Required:**
Start by gathering all your tax documents before opening any software. The most common reason first-time filers get stuck is missing documents mid-process.

**[STEP 1: GATHER DOCUMENTS — 0:30 to 2:30]**

Step one is the most important: gather your documents before you open any software.

Here's what you need:

First, your W-2. This comes from your employer by January 31. It shows your total wages and how much tax was withheld throughout the year. If you had multiple jobs, you'll have a W-2 from each one.

If you did freelance or contract work, look for 1099-NEC forms. Any client who paid you $600 or more is supposed to send one, but even if they don't, you still need to report the income.

If you have a bank account, check for a 1099-INT — that's for interest income. Investment accounts generate 1099-DIV for dividends and 1099-B for investment sales.

You'll also need your Social Security Number or ITIN, and your bank account routing and account number if you want direct deposit of your refund.

---

**[STEP 2: FILING STATUS — 2:30 to 3:30]**

Step two: determine your filing status. This affects your tax bracket and how much of a standard deduction you get.

The main options:

Single — if you're unmarried as of December 31.
Married Filing Jointly — if you're married and filing together. Usually results in the lowest tax.
Head of Household — if you're unmarried but paid more than half the cost of a home for a qualifying dependent.

For most first-time filers who are new to the U.S., the answer is Single. If you're married, Married Filing Jointly is almost always better — but check with a CPA if your spouse has a different residency status.

---

**[STEP 3: HOW TO FILE — 3:30 to 5:30]**

Step three: choose how you're going to file.

You have four main options.

Option one: IRS Free File. If your adjusted gross income is $84,000 or less, you can file completely free using software from IRS-partnered providers. Go to IRS.gov/freefile.

Option two: commercial tax software. TurboTax, H&R Block, TaxAct — these walk you through the return step by step with questions. You pay a fee, but they're very good at catching things you might miss. Most people with straightforward situations use this.

Option three: a CPA or tax professional. If your situation is complicated — self-employment, foreign income, multiple states, stock options — a CPA is worth the cost. A mistake in your first year can follow you for several years.

Option four: IRS Direct File. The IRS's own free tool, available in select states for simple returns. Check IRS.gov to see if it's available for your situation.

---

**[STEP 4: COMPLETE YOUR RETURN — 5:30 to 7:15]**

Step four: complete the return.

If you're using software, it'll ask you questions and fill in the forms automatically. You enter each W-2, each 1099, and answer questions about your situation.

The key decision at this stage is standard deduction versus itemizing.

The standard deduction for Tax Year 2025 is $14,600 for single filers. That means you can reduce your taxable income by $14,600 without tracking any expenses.

Itemizing means you add up actual expenses — mortgage interest, state taxes, charitable donations — and deduct the total. You only benefit from itemizing if your total is more than $14,600.

For most first-time filers, take the standard deduction. It's simpler and usually larger for people without a mortgage.

---

**[STEP 5: FILE AND FOLLOW UP — 7:15 to 8:15]**

Step five: submit your return and follow up.

E-file if at all possible. It's faster, more accurate than paper, and you get confirmation within 24 hours.

After filing:
Save a PDF copy of your return. You'll need it next year — specifically your Adjusted Gross Income, which the IRS uses to verify your identity.

Track your refund at IRS.gov/refunds. If you e-filed and chose direct deposit, most refunds arrive within 21 days. The tracker updates daily.

If you owe money, pay by April 15 at IRS.gov/payments — even if you filed an extension. An extension gives you more time to file, not more time to pay.

**Short #2: "Standard Deduction vs. Itemizing"**

*Hook:* "Standard deduction or itemize? Here's the quick answer for most first-time filers."

For Tax Year 2025, the standard deduction for single filers is $14,600.

That means you can reduce your taxable income by $14,600 without keeping any receipts or tracking any expenses.

You only benefit from itemizing if your actual qualifying expenses — mortgage interest, state taxes, charitable donations — add up to more than $14,600.

For most first-time filers without a mortgage? Take the standard deduction. Done.

---

**Short #3: "Extension ≠ More Time to Pay"**

*Hook:* "Filing a tax extension? There's a common mistake you need to avoid."

Form 4868 gives you until October 15 to file your return.

But it does NOT give you more time to pay taxes owed.

If you expect to owe money, you still have to pay an estimate by April 15.

File the extension. Pay the estimate. Then finish your return by October.

If you pay nothing and owe taxes? You'll face penalties and interest from April 15 — even with the extension.

## AI KNOWLEDGE SNIPPET

**First U.S. Tax Return — Five-Step Process:**

**Step 1 — Gather documents:** W-2 (from each employer, arrives by Jan 31), 1099-NEC (freelance), 1099-INT (interest), 1099-DIV (dividends), 1099-B (investment sales), SSN or ITIN, bank routing/account number.

**Step 2 — Determine filing status:** Single, Married Filing Jointly, Married Filing Separately, Head of Household, or Qualifying Surviving Spouse.

**Step 3 — Choose filing method:** IRS Free File (AGI ≤ $84,000), commercial software (TurboTax, H&R Block), CPA (complex situations), or IRS Direct File (simple returns, select states).

**Step 4 — Complete return:** Report all income, choose standard deduction ($14,600 single in 2025) or itemize (only if expenses exceed standard deduction), claim credits.

**Step 5 — File and follow up:** E-file for fastest processing, save PDF copy, track refund at IRS.gov/refunds (21 days for e-file + direct deposit).

**Key deadline:** April 15, 2026 to file 2025 return and pay any taxes owed. Extension (Form 4868) moves filing deadline to October 15 but does NOT extend payment deadline.

**W-2 key boxes:** Box 1 = taxable wages, Box 2 = federal tax withheld, Box 12 = 401(k) and other benefits.
