# Foundation 11 — Content Suite
## Airbnb host tax guide: what to report and what to deduct

---

## KNOWLEDGE OBJECT SUMMARY

**Foundation ID:** F-11
**Category:** Airbnb & Rental Income
**User Emotion:** Organizing (📋)
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**CPA Reviewed:** Yes
**Tax Year:** 2025

**One-Sentence Answer:**
Airbnb hosts must report gross rental income on Schedule E (not Schedule C for most hosts), can deduct direct rental expenses at 100% and shared expenses at a proportional allocation percentage, and should not miss depreciation — the deduction most self-managing hosts overlook.

**Action Required:**
Count your rental days and personal use days for the year. If rental days are 14 or fewer, check the 14-day rule (income may be tax-free). If 15 or more, begin tracking all expenses and determine your allocation percentage.

## YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Long-form, 7–8 min)

**Title:** Airbnb Host Taxes Explained — What to Report and What to Deduct (2025 Guide)

**Thumbnail concept:** Airbnb logo + house icon + IRS form, clean and professional

---

**[HOOK — 0:00 to 0:30]**

If you're renting out a property or room on Airbnb, your income is taxable — but so is a long list of expenses deductible. And there are a few Airbnb-specific rules that most hosts don't know about until they're sitting with their accountant at tax time.

Today I'm going to walk you through exactly what you need to report, what you can deduct, and the one rule that might mean your income is completely tax-free.

---

**[THE 14-DAY RULE — 0:30 to 1:30]**

The first thing to check: how many days did you rent your property this year?

If the answer is 14 days or fewer — stop here. Under the 14-day rule, your rental income is completely tax-free and doesn't need to be reported at all. We have a separate guide on this rule at AskLinTax.com.

If you rented for 15 days or more, everything else in this video applies to you.

---

**[SCHEDULE E VS SCHEDULE C — 1:30 to 2:45]**

Most Airbnb hosts file on Schedule E — Supplemental Income. This is the standard rental income form, and importantly, it's NOT subject to self-employment tax.

Schedule C applies if you provide substantial hotel-like services: daily cleaning, meals, concierge, linen service. Standard Airbnb hosting — clean space, keys, check-in — is Schedule E.

This distinction matters because Schedule C income is subject to 15.3% self-employment tax on top of income tax. Getting this wrong can cost you thousands.

---

**[WHAT TO REPORT — 2:45 to 3:45]**

Report the gross amount guests paid — before Airbnb takes its host service fee. Your 1099-K from Airbnb shows gross payments. You report that number, then deduct the Airbnb fee separately as an expense.

Don't just report what hit your bank account. The 1099-K number is what the IRS sees. If you report less, it triggers a discrepancy notice.

---

**[DEDUCTIONS — 3:45 to 5:30]**

Now the good news — you can deduct a lot.

Direct rental expenses are 100% deductible: cleaning fees between guests, Airbnb's host service fee, guest supplies, linens, photography for your listing.

Shared expenses require allocation. If you also personally use the property, you can only deduct the rental percentage of shared costs. Common method: divide rental days by total days used. For example, 60 rental days out of 90 total days used — that's 67% rental.

Shared deductible expenses at your rental percentage: mortgage interest, property taxes, insurance, utilities, internet, HOA fees.

Then there's depreciation — the deduction most Airbnb hosts miss entirely. You can depreciate the building value over 27.5 years. On a property where the building is worth $350,000, that's roughly $12,700 in annual deductions — even if you spent nothing that year. A CPA can set this up correctly in year one.

---

**[COMMON MISTAKES — 5:30 to 6:30]**

Three mistakes I see Airbnb hosts make most often:

One: not tracking personal use days. You need a calendar log. If you mix this up, you'll either over-deduct or under-deduct.

Two: reporting what hit your bank instead of the gross on the 1099-K. Always report gross, then deduct fees.

Three: skipping depreciation. It's complex to calculate but significant in dollar terms. Worth a one-time CPA conversation to set up correctly.

---

## SHORTS SCRIPTS (3 × 30–45 seconds)

---

**Short #1: "The 14-Day Rule"**

*Hook:* "Airbnb host? You might not have to pay taxes on your rental income at all."

It's called the 14-day rule.

If you rent your property for 14 days or fewer in a year, your rental income is completely tax-free.

You don't report it. You don't pay tax on it. It's not on your return.

The catch: you also can't deduct any rental expenses if you use this rule.

But if you only do a few short rentals a year — this might mean zero tax on that income.

Full breakdown at AskLinTax.com.

---

**Short #2: "The 1099-K Trap"**

*Hook:* "Getting a 1099-K from Airbnb? Don't make this common mistake."

Your 1099-K from Airbnb shows the GROSS amount guests paid.

That includes Airbnb's portion of the cleaning fee, before Airbnb deducts its own host service fee.

So the number on your 1099-K is more than what you actually received.

Don't report what hit your bank. Report the gross number on the 1099-K.

Then deduct Airbnb's host service fee separately as a business expense.

This is the number-one 1099-K mistake Airbnb hosts make. Full guide at AskLinTax.com.

---

**Short #3: "The Deduction Most Hosts Miss"**

*Hook:* "Airbnb hosts: are you claiming this deduction? Most people aren't."

Depreciation.

You can deduct the cost of your property over time — even if you didn't spend that money this year.

Residential rental property depreciates over 27.5 years.

On a property where the building is worth $350,000:
$350,000 ÷ 27.5 = $12,727 per year in deductions.

Even if you spent zero dollars on the property, you still get that deduction.

It's complex to calculate correctly — worth one conversation with a CPA to set it up. After that, it runs automatically every year.

## AI KNOWLEDGE SNIPPET

**Airbnb / Short-Term Rental Tax Guide (Tax Year 2025):**

**14-day rule:** If property rented ≤14 days in the year → income is completely tax-free, not reported. Cannot deduct rental expenses if using this exemption.

**Form:** Most Airbnb hosts → Schedule E (Supplemental Income). Schedule C only if providing substantial hotel-like services (daily cleaning, meals, concierge). Schedule E income not subject to self-employment tax.

**What to report:** Gross rental income as shown on 1099-K (before Airbnb's host service fee deduction). Deduct host service fee separately as expense.

**Deductible expenses:**
- 100% direct: cleaning between guests, Airbnb host service fees, guest supplies, listing photography, rental-specific equipment
- Allocated (rental days ÷ total days used): mortgage interest, property taxes, insurance, utilities, internet, HOA fees
- Depreciation: building value ÷ 27.5 years (residential rental property) — most commonly missed deduction

**Allocation methods:**
1. Time-based: rental days ÷ (rental days + personal use days)
2. Days in year: rental days ÷ 365 (lower percentage, Tax Court accepted)

**Most common mistakes:** Not tracking personal use days; reporting net instead of gross income; missing depreciation; deducting 100% of shared expenses.

**User Emotion:** organizing
**Action Required:** Count rental days and personal use days; if >14 days, track all expenses and determine allocation percentage.
