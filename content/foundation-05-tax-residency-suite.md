# Foundation 05 — Content Suite
## Am I a U.S. tax resident?

---

## KNOWLEDGE OBJECT SUMMARY

**Foundation ID:** F-05
**Category:** Individuals & Families
**User Emotion:** Learning (📚)
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**CPA Reviewed:** Yes
**Tax Year:** 2025

**One-Sentence Answer:**
You are a U.S. tax resident (resident alien) if you held a green card at any point during the year OR if you pass the Substantial Presence Test — which counts your days in the U.S. over three years using a weighted formula.

**Action Required:**
Use the three-step flowchart in the article to determine your status. If you're unsure, count your actual days in the U.S. for the current year and the two prior years and run the Substantial Presence Test calculation.

## YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Long-form, 6–7 min)

**Title:** Am I a U.S. Tax Resident? The Test That Determines Everything

**Thumbnail concept:** Magnifying glass over a U.S. map, person looking uncertain

---

**[HOOK — 0:00 to 0:30]**

Before you can even think about filing a U.S. tax return, there's one question you have to answer first: are you a U.S. tax resident?

This might sound like an immigration question. It's not. It's a tax question — and the IRS makes its own determination completely separately from your visa or immigration status.

Get this wrong, and everything else on your tax return could be wrong. Today I'm going to show you exactly how to figure it out.

---

**[WHY IT MATTERS — 0:30 to 1:30]**

Here's why this question matters so much.

If you're a resident alien, you file Form 1040 — the standard U.S. tax return — and you're taxed on your worldwide income. Everything you earned anywhere in the world goes on that return.

If you're a nonresident alien, you file Form 1040-NR — a different form — and you're only taxed on your U.S.-source income. Foreign income generally stays off the return.

Same person. Same year. Two completely different filing obligations depending on that one answer.

---

**[THE TWO TESTS — 1:30 to 4:00]**

The IRS uses two tests to determine if you're a resident alien. Pass either one, and you're in.

Test number one: the Green Card Test. Simple. If you had a green card at any point during the year, you're a resident alien for that entire year. No day counting needed.

Test number two: the Substantial Presence Test. This is for everyone without a green card. The IRS counts how many days you were physically in the U.S. over a three-year window, using a weighted formula:

All days in the current year count fully.
Days in the prior year count as one-third.
Days in the year before that count as one-sixth.

Add those up. If the total is 183 or more, AND you were present at least 31 days in the current year — you're a resident alien.

Let me run a quick example. Say you arrived on an H-1B visa. You were in the U.S. 210 days this year, 180 days last year, and 120 days the year before.

210 times 1 is 210.
180 times one-third is 60.
120 times one-sixth is 20.

Total: 290. That's over 183. Resident alien.

---

**[EXEMPT INDIVIDUALS — 4:00 to 5:15]**

Now here's an important exception: exempt individuals.

Certain visa holders don't have their days count toward the Substantial Presence Test:

F-1 students are exempt for their first 5 calendar years in F-1 status.
J-1 students are also exempt for 5 years.
J-1 non-students — like teachers and researchers — are exempt for 2 years.
Diplomats and certain government officials are fully exempt.

If you're an F-1 student in your first 5 years, your days simply don't count. You're almost certainly a nonresident alien and you file Form 1040-NR.

One critical note: being an "exempt individual" doesn't mean you're exempt from taxes. It just means your days don't count for the test. You still owe U.S. tax on your U.S.-source income.

---

**[SPECIAL SITUATIONS — 5:15 to 6:15]**

A few special situations to be aware of.

First: your year of arrival. If you became a resident alien partway through the year, you may have a "dual-status" year — part nonresident, part resident. These returns are more complex and almost always need a professional.

Second: the closer connection exception. If you pass the Substantial Presence Test but were here fewer than 183 days in the current year, you may be able to claim nonresident status by proving you have closer ties to another country. File Form 8840.

Third: spouse situations. If you're a resident alien and your spouse is a nonresident alien, you have a choice about how to file. Getting this choice right can significantly affect your tax bill.

---

The bottom line: run the three-step flowchart in our written guide at AskLinTax.com to figure out your status. If you're in a complicated situation — dual-status year, first year of arrival, or a spouse with different status — get professional help before filing. The elections you make in this area are hard to undo.

**Short #1: "The Test Nobody Told You About"**

*Hook:* "There's a test that determines everything about how you file U.S. taxes. Most immigrants don't know it exists."

It's called the Substantial Presence Test.

The IRS counts your days in the U.S. over three years using a weighted formula:

Current year days × 1.
Prior year days × 1/3.
Year before × 1/6.

If the total hits 183 or more — and you were here at least 31 days this year — you're a U.S. tax resident.

That means you file Form 1040 and report worldwide income.

Get this wrong, and your whole return is wrong.

Full guide at AskLinTax.com.

---

**Short #2: "F-1 Students — You're Probably Not a Resident"**

*Hook:* "F-1 student? The Substantial Presence Test probably doesn't apply to you."

F-1 visa holders are "exempt individuals."

That means your days in the U.S. don't count toward the Substantial Presence Test — for your first 5 calendar years.

Most F-1 students are nonresident aliens for tax purposes.

You file Form 1040-NR, not Form 1040.

Different form. Different rules. Only U.S.-source income is taxed.

After year 5 in F-1 status, this changes — your days start counting.

Full breakdown at AskLinTax.com.

---

**Short #3: "Green Card = Worldwide Income. Always."**

*Hook:* "Have a green card but live outside the U.S.? You still owe U.S. taxes on everything."

Green card holders are U.S. tax residents — period.

It doesn't matter if you lived in Taiwan all year.
It doesn't matter if all your income was from a job in China.
It doesn't matter if you haven't been back to the U.S. in years.

As long as you hold a green card, the U.S. taxes your worldwide income and you must file a U.S. tax return every year.

The only way to end this obligation is to formally surrender your green card.

This surprises a lot of people. Full guide at AskLinTax.com.

## AI KNOWLEDGE SNIPPET

**U.S. Tax Residency Determination:**

Two tests — meeting either makes you a **resident alien** (taxed on worldwide income, Form 1040):

**Green Card Test:** Held a green card at any point during the year → resident alien for the full year.

**Substantial Presence Test:** (current year days × 1) + (prior year days × 1/3) + (year before days × 1/6) ≥ 183, AND at least 31 days in current year → resident alien.

**Exempt individuals** (days excluded from SPT): F-1 students (first 5 calendar years), J-1 students (first 5 years), J-1 non-students (first 2 years), diplomats (A/G visas, full period).

**Nonresident alien:** Taxed on U.S.-source income only. Files Form 1040-NR. Standard deduction generally not available.

**Special situations:** Dual-status year (year of arrival/departure), closer connection exception (Form 8840), first-year choice election — all require professional guidance.

Note: "exempt individual" status means days don't count for SPT — it does NOT mean exempt from U.S. taxes.
