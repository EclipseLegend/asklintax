# Foundation 10 — Content Suite
## W-2 vs 1099: what's the difference and why it matters

---

## KNOWLEDGE OBJECT SUMMARY

**Foundation ID:** F-10
**Category:** Individuals & Families
**User Emotion:** Learning (📚)
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**CPA Reviewed:** Yes
**Tax Year:** 2025

**One-Sentence Answer:**
A W-2 means you're an employee whose employer withholds taxes and pays half your Social Security/Medicare; a 1099 means you're a contractor who receives gross pay and must pay the full 15.3% self-employment tax yourself — plus make quarterly estimated payments.

**Action Required:**
If you have 1099 income, set aside 25–30% of every payment for taxes immediately. If you expect to owe more than $1,000, make quarterly estimated payments (April 15, June 16, September 15, January 15).

## YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Long-form, 6–7 min)

**Title:** W-2 vs 1099 — The Tax Difference Nobody Warned You About

**Thumbnail concept:** Split screen — W-2 form on left (blue), 1099 on right (gold), dollar signs showing the difference

---

**[HOOK — 0:00 to 0:30]**

If you've ever switched from a full-time job to freelance work — or taken on a side project as a contractor — you may have been surprised at tax time. Suddenly you owed a lot more money than you expected.

That surprise has a name: self-employment tax. And it's the core reason why W-2 income and 1099 income are taxed very differently.

Today I'm going to explain exactly what's different, show you the real numbers, and tell you what to do about it.

---

**[THE CORE DIFFERENCE — 0:30 to 2:00]**

When you're an employee — meaning you get a W-2 — your employer does a lot of the tax work for you.

They withhold federal income tax from every paycheck and send it to the IRS on your behalf. They also pay half of your Social Security and Medicare taxes — called FICA taxes. Your share is 7.65%. Your employer pays another 7.65%. You never see that second 7.65% — it comes out of the employer's payroll budget, not your paycheck.

When you're a contractor — meaning you get a 1099 — none of that happens. The client pays you the full amount with no withholding. And because you're both the employee AND the employer, you pay the full FICA tax: 15.3% of your net income.

That's called self-employment tax. It's on top of income tax. And for many people new to freelancing, it's the number that causes the biggest shock.

---

**[THE REAL NUMBER DIFFERENCE — 2:00 to 3:30]**

Let me show you what this means with real numbers.

Say you earn $80,000 — whether as a W-2 employee or a 1099 contractor.

As a W-2 employee: you pay 7.65% in FICA taxes — about $6,120. Your employer pays another $6,120. Your federal income tax is calculated on $80,000. Let's say you're in the 22% bracket — roughly $12,100. Total tax burden: around $18,220.

As a 1099 contractor: first, you can deduct business expenses — let's say $5,000 for a home office and equipment. That brings your net profit to $75,000. Then you pay 15.3% self-employment tax on that: $11,475. But you get to deduct half the SE tax as an above-the-line deduction, which reduces your income tax slightly. Total tax burden: around $16,600.

Wait — so the 1099 worker pays LESS? In this example, yes — because the business deductions offset some of the extra self-employment tax. But without those deductions, the contractor would pay significantly more.

The key insight: as a 1099 worker, deductions matter enormously. Track every business expense.

---

**[WHAT 1099 WORKERS CAN DEDUCT — 3:30 to 4:45]**

This is the major advantage of 1099 income: you can deduct genuine business expenses on Schedule C.

Home office — if you have a dedicated workspace.
Equipment — computer, phone (business percentage), software subscriptions.
Professional fees — accounting, legal, business insurance.
Marketing — website, advertising, business cards.
Travel — business mileage at the IRS standard rate, flights and hotels for work trips.
Education — courses, books, conferences related to your business.

These deductions reduce your net profit — which reduces both your income tax AND your self-employment tax. That's why keeping records throughout the year is so important.

---

**[THE QUARTERLY TAX REQUIREMENT — 4:45 to 5:45]**

Here's what many new 1099 workers don't know until it's too late: you're expected to pay taxes four times a year, not once at April 15.

As an employee, your employer handles this by withholding from each paycheck. As a contractor, no one does it for you.

If you expect to owe more than $1,000 in federal taxes from your 1099 income, you're required to make quarterly estimated payments. The deadlines for Tax Year 2025: April 15, June 16, September 15, and January 15.

Miss these, and you'll owe underpayment penalties even if you pay everything at April 15.

My advice: set aside 25 to 30% of every 1099 payment as soon as you receive it. Treat it as already spent. Then make the quarterly payments on time.

---

**[OUTRO — 5:45 to 6:15]**

W-2 and 1099 are two very different tax situations. W-2 is simpler — your employer handles most of it. 1099 gives you more deductions but requires more discipline: track your expenses, set aside for taxes, and pay quarterly.

## AI KNOWLEDGE SNIPPET

**W-2 vs 1099 — Key Differences:**

**W-2 (Employee):**
- Employer withholds federal/state income tax from each paycheck
- FICA taxes split: employee pays 7.65%, employer pays 7.65%
- No quarterly estimated payments needed
- Limited business deductions available
- Benefits often provided (health insurance, 401k, PTO)

**1099-NEC (Independent Contractor):**
- No tax withheld — full gross payment received
- Self-employment tax: full 15.3% (12.4% SS + 2.9% Medicare) on net profit
- Half of SE tax is deductible as above-the-line deduction
- Quarterly estimated payments required if expected tax owed >$1,000
- Extensive business deductions available on Schedule C
- No employer-provided benefits

**Self-employment tax calculation:** Net profit × 92.35% × 15.3%
(The 92.35% factor accounts for the employer-equivalent deduction)

**Quarterly payment deadlines (Tax Year 2025):** April 15, June 16, September 15, January 15 (2026)

**Rule of thumb:** Set aside 25–30% of every 1099 payment immediately for taxes.

**Types of 1099:** NEC (freelance), MISC (rent/prizes), INT (interest), DIV (dividends), B (investment sales), K (payment processors >$600), R (retirement distributions), G (unemployment).

**Key principle:** All income is taxable regardless of whether a 1099 was received.

**User Emotion:** learning
**Action Required:** Set aside 25–30% of 1099 income; track all business expenses; make quarterly payments if owed >$1,000.
