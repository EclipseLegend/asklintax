## Quarterly estimated taxes: who pays and how to calculate

---

## KNOWLEDGE OBJECT SUMMARY

**Foundation ID:** F-13
**Category:** Small Business & Self-Employment
**User Emotion:** Organizing (📋)
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**CPA Reviewed:** Yes
**Tax Year:** 2025

**One-Sentence Answer:**
If you expect to owe more than $1,000 in federal taxes from self-employment or other non-withheld income, you must make quarterly estimated tax payments on April 15, June 16, September 15, and January 15 — or face underpayment penalties even if you pay everything in April.

**Action Required:**
Mark the four quarterly deadlines in your calendar. Set up a separate savings account for tax reserves and transfer 25–30% of every income payment into it immediately. Make your first quarterly payment on time — penalties apply even for a single missed quarter.

## YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Long-form, 6–7 min)

**Title:** Quarterly Estimated Taxes Explained — Who Pays, How Much, and When (2025–2026)

**Thumbnail concept:** Calendar with Q1–Q4 highlighted, "Don't Miss These Dates" label

---

**[HOOK — 0:00 to 0:30]**

One of the biggest financial surprises for people new to self-employment is discovering — usually in April — that they owe thousands of dollars in taxes, plus a penalty for not paying throughout the year.

The penalty applies even if you pay everything in full by April 15. Because the IRS expects tax payments four times a year, not once.

Today I'm going to explain exactly who needs to make quarterly payments, how to calculate them, and the simplest system for making sure you always have the money ready.

---

**[WHY QUARTERLY PAYMENTS EXIST — 0:30 to 1:15]**

The U.S. tax system is "pay as you go."

When you're an employee, your employer handles this automatically by withholding taxes from each paycheck. You never think about it.

When you're self-employed, a freelancer, or an LLC owner, no one withholds anything. Every dollar arrives gross. The IRS still expects to receive taxes throughout the year — which is why quarterly estimated payments exist.

---

**[WHO NEEDS TO PAY — 1:15 to 2:15]**

You need to make quarterly payments if:

One — you expect to owe at least $1,000 in federal taxes from non-withheld income.

Two — your withholding from a W-2 job (if you have one) doesn't cover 90% of your current year tax liability or 100% of last year's.

If you're a freelancer with no W-2 job — you almost certainly need to make quarterly payments from your very first quarter of income.

If you have a W-2 job plus side income, you might be able to increase your W-4 withholding instead of making separate quarterly payments. It depends on how much the side income is.

---

**[THE DEADLINES — 2:15 to 3:00]**

For Tax Year 2025 income:

Q1 — April 15, 2026. Same day as the annual filing deadline.
Q2 — June 16, 2026. Only about two months after Q1 — it comes fast.
Q3 — September 15, 2026.
Q4 — January 15, 2027.

Mark all four in your calendar right now.

---

**[HOW TO CALCULATE — 3:00 to 4:30]**

Two methods. Choose one.

Method one: the safe harbor method. Take last year's total tax bill and divide by four. Pay that amount each quarter. If you do this, you're guaranteed no underpayment penalty — regardless of what you actually earn this year. Best for people with variable or unpredictable income.

Method two: estimate your current year income. Take your expected annual net profit — revenue minus business expenses. Calculate self-employment tax: net profit times 92.35%, times 15.3%. Then calculate income tax on the net profit minus half the SE tax. Add both taxes together and divide by four.

Let me give you a quick example. Say you expect to net $80,000 this year.

Self-employment tax: $80,000 × 0.9235 × 15.3% = about $11,300.
You can deduct half of that — about $5,650 — reducing taxable income to $74,350.
Income tax at roughly 22%: about $16,400.
Total annual tax: about $27,700.
Divided by four: about $6,925 per quarter.

---

**[HOW TO PAY — 4:30 to 5:30]**

The easiest way: IRS Direct Pay at IRS.gov/payments. Free, instant, no account required. Pay directly from your bank account. You get immediate confirmation.

If you want to schedule payments in advance, set up an account at EFTPS.gov — the Electronic Federal Tax Payment System. Free, but takes a few days to activate.

Don't mail a check unless you have to. No instant confirmation, and it can get lost.

---

**[THE SYSTEM — 5:30 to 6:15]**

The simplest system I've seen work for self-employed people:

Open a separate savings account. Label it "Tax Reserve."

Every time you receive a payment, immediately transfer 25–30% into that account. Treat it as if the money was never yours.

When quarterly time comes, the money is there. No scramble, no stress, no borrowing from other accounts.

Whatever is left in the tax reserve after you file in April? That's your bonus.

**Quarterly Estimated Tax Payments (Federal, Tax Year 2025):**

**Who must pay:** Taxpayers who expect to owe ≥$1,000 in federal taxes from non-withheld income AND whose withholding covers less than 90% of current year liability or 100% of prior year liability (110% if prior year AGI >$150,000).

**2026 deadlines for Tax Year 2025:**
- Q1 (Jan–Mar 2025): April 15, 2026
- Q2 (Apr–May 2025): June 16, 2026
- Q3 (Jun–Aug 2025): September 15, 2026
- Q4 (Sep–Dec 2025): January 15, 2027

**Penalty:** Underpayment penalty charged quarterly at federal short-term rate +3%. Applies even if full amount paid by April 15.

**Calculation methods:**
1. Safe harbor: Pay 100% of prior year tax liability ÷ 4 (110% if prior year AGI >$150,000) → guaranteed no penalty
2. Current year estimate: (SE tax + income tax on net profit) ÷ 4

**SE tax calculation:** Net profit × 92.35% × 15.3% (deduct half of SE tax from income before calculating income tax)

**How to pay:** IRS Direct Pay (IRS.gov/payments) — free, instant, recommended. EFTPS for advance scheduling. Check with Form 1040-ES as last resort.

**Q4 exception:** Can skip January 15 payment if complete return filed and balance paid by January 31.

**Best practice:** Separate "tax reserve" savings account; transfer 25–30% of every income payment immediately.
