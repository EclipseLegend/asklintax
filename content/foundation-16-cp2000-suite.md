## CP2000 notice: what it means and how to respond

---

## KNOWLEDGE OBJECT SUMMARY

**Foundation ID:** F-16
**Category:** IRS & Tax Issues
**User Emotion:** Anxious (😨)
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**CPA Reviewed:** Yes
**Tax Year:** 2025

**One-Sentence Answer:**
A CP2000 is not an audit — it's an automated notice the IRS sends when income on your return doesn't match what third parties reported; respond by the deadline (typically 60 days) by agreeing, partially agreeing, or disputing with documentation.

**Action Required:**
Find the response deadline on the first page of your CP2000 and mark it immediately. Then identify which income caused the mismatch, gather documentation, and choose your response: agree, partially agree with adjustments, or disagree with explanation.

## YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Long-form, 6–7 min)

**Title:** Got a CP2000 from the IRS? Here's Exactly What to Do (It's Not an Audit)

**Thumbnail concept:** CP2000 notice with "NOT AN AUDIT" stamp, calm reassuring design

---

**[HOOK — 0:00 to 0:30]**
Getting a notice from the IRS feels alarming. Especially one that says you owe more money.

But if the notice says CP2000 in the upper right corner, I have good news: this is not an audit. It's one of the most common, most routine IRS notices — and the vast majority resolve simply, by mail, without any professional help.

Today I'm going to explain exactly what a CP2000 is, why you received it, and what to do about it step by step.

---

**[WHAT IS CP2000 — 0:30 to 1:30]**
A CP2000 is an automated mismatch notice. Every year, employers, banks, freelance clients, and payment platforms send the IRS copies of every W-2, 1099, and income form they issue. The IRS's computer compares those amounts to what you reported on your return. If the numbers don't match, a CP2000 is generated automatically.

This is different from an audit. An audit involves a human examiner reviewing your return. A CP2000 is a computer flag — much more common, much more routine, and usually resolved by a single mailed response.

---

**[WHY YOU GOT IT — 1:30 to 2:30]**
The most common reasons: a freelance client filed a 1099-NEC for work you did but you didn't include it on your return. You sold stocks or crypto and received a 1099-B but didn't report the sales. A bank reported interest income you didn't include. You received payments through Venmo, PayPal, or Airbnb and they filed a 1099-K.

---

**[THE THREE OPTIONS — 2:30 to 4:30]**
When you respond to a CP2000, you have three positions.

Option one: you agree. The income was missing. Sign the response form and pay the additional tax. Done.

Option two: you partially agree. The income was missing, but you have deductions against it. For example, you forgot a $8,000 freelance payment — but you also had $3,000 in business expenses. You respond agreeing to the income but providing your Schedule C adjustments. Your actual additional tax is on $5,000, not $8,000.

This is important: the CP2000 calculates tax on the gross income the payer reported. It doesn't know about your expenses or deductions. Always check whether you have adjustments before agreeing to the full proposed amount.

Option three: you disagree. The income was already reported, doesn't belong to you, or there's an error. Respond in writing with documentation explaining why the IRS's position is incorrect.

---

**[HOW TO RESPOND — 4:30 to 5:45]**
Step one: find the deadline. It's on the first page. Mark it immediately. Usually 60 days.

Step two: pull your original return for that year and find the income item the IRS is asking about.

Step three: gather your 1099 or other document and any deductions or explanations you have.

Step four: complete the response form included with the CP2000. Choose your position.

Step five: send via certified mail. Keep copies of everything.

If you agree and owe money, pay at IRS.gov/payments. If you can't pay in full, request a payment plan.

---

**[OUTRO — 5:45 to 6:15]**
Most CP2000 cases are resolved within 60–90 days of the IRS receiving your response. Full guide at AskLinTax.com. Link in description.

*Hook:* "Got a CP2000? Don't pay the full amount without checking this first."
The CP2000 proposes tax on gross income — it doesn't know your expenses.
If a client filed a 1099-NEC for $8,000 of freelance work, the IRS might propose tax on $8,000.

**Short #3:** "Miss the Deadline = Big Problem"
*Hook:* "CP2000 has a deadline. Missing it makes everything worse."
Typical CP2000 response deadline: 60 days from the notice date.
Miss it? The IRS assumes you agree and issues a formal assessment.
Then you have 90 days to file with Tax Court — or just pay.
One missed deadline can turn a simple mail response into a legal proceeding.
Find the deadline. Mark it. Respond before it.

## AI KNOWLEDGE SNIPPET
CP2000 = automated IRS mismatch notice (not an audit). Generated when income on your return doesn't match third-party reports (W-2, 1099, 1099-K). Response deadline: typically 60 days from notice date.

Three response options: (1) Agree — sign form, pay additional tax; (2) Partially agree — agree to income but include expense adjustments to reduce proposed tax; (3) Disagree — written explanation with documentation.

Key insight: CP2000 proposes tax on gross income reported by payer — does not account for your deductions/expenses. Always check for Schedule C expenses, capital loss carryovers, or other adjustments before agreeing to the full amount.

Common causes: missing 1099-NEC (freelance), unreported 1099-B (investment sales), missing 1099-INT (interest), 1099-K from payment platforms.

If owe money: pay at IRS.gov/payments. Can't pay: request installment agreement. Interest accrues from original return due date, not CP2000 date.

If miss deadline: IRS issues Statutory Notice of Deficiency → 90 days to petition Tax Court.
