# Foundation 06 — Content Suite
## Do I need to file a U.S. tax return?

---

## KNOWLEDGE OBJECT SUMMARY

**Foundation ID:** F-06
**Category:** Individuals & Families
**User Emotion:** Learning (📚)
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**CPA Reviewed:** Yes
**Tax Year:** 2025

**One-Sentence Answer:**
You are required to file a U.S. federal tax return if your gross income exceeds the standard deduction for your filing status ($14,600 for single filers under 65 in 2025) — but you should often file even if you're not required to, especially if taxes were withheld or you qualify for refundable credits.

**Action Required:**
Check your gross income against the threshold for your filing status. If it exceeds the threshold, you must file. If it doesn't, check whether you had taxes withheld or qualify for refundable credits — if so, file anyway to claim your refund.

## YOUTUBE SCRIPT (Long-form, 5–6 min)

**Title:** Do I Need to File a U.S. Tax Return? (How to Know for Sure)

**Thumbnail concept:** Checklist on a desk, question mark, person looking thoughtful

---

**[HOOK — 0:00 to 0:25]**

One of the most common questions I get — especially from people new to the U.S. tax system — is: "Do I even need to file a tax return?"

And the answer isn't always obvious, because "required to file" and "should file" are two very different things.

Today I'm going to give you both answers — clearly, in five minutes.

---

**[THE BASIC RULE — 0:25 to 1:30]**

The basic rule is simple. You are required to file a U.S. federal tax return if your gross income exceeds the filing threshold for your filing status.

For Tax Year 2025, here are the key numbers:

Single filer under 65: $14,600
Married Filing Jointly, both under 65: $29,200
Head of Household under 65: $21,900

These numbers are based on the standard deduction. If your income is below your threshold, you are not legally required to file.

But — and this is important — "not required" doesn't mean "shouldn't file."

---

**[WHEN TO FILE ANYWAY — 1:30 to 3:00]**

Here are four situations where you should file even if you're not required to:

Number one: taxes were withheld from your paycheck. If your employer withheld federal income tax throughout the year, the only way to get that money back is to file a return. Many people with income below the threshold still receive refunds.

Number two: you qualify for refundable credits. Credits like the Earned Income Tax Credit and the Additional Child Tax Credit can put money in your pocket even if you owe no taxes — but only if you file.

Number three: you want to build a filing history. Consistent tax filing is often required for mortgage applications, visa renewals, and green card applications. Even voluntary filings create a useful financial record.

Number four: you want to contribute to an IRA. To make IRA contributions based on earned income, you need to have filed for that year.

---

**[SPECIAL CASES — 3:00 to 4:15]**

A few special situations always require filing, regardless of income level:

Self-employment income of $400 or more — always requires filing because self-employment tax is owed.

Nonresident aliens — if you're on an F-1 or J-1 visa and haven't been in the U.S. long enough to be a resident alien, different rules apply. You file Form 1040-NR, not the standard Form 1040. And F-1 students with no U.S. income still need to file Form 8843 every year.

Gifts from parents abroad — money sent by parents in Taiwan or China is generally not taxable income and doesn't count toward the filing threshold. But if you received more than $100,000 in a year from foreign individuals, you must file Form 3520 to report it.

---

**[THE BOTTOM LINE — 4:15 to 5:00]**

The cost of filing when you didn't need to: a few hours of your time.

The cost of not filing when you were required to: failure-to-file penalties of 5% per month, plus interest, up to 25% of unpaid taxes.

When in doubt, file. And if any taxes were withheld from any income you earned this year — always file. It's likely the only way to get that money back.

## SHORTS SCRIPTS (3 × 30–45 seconds)

---

**Short #1: "The $14,600 Rule"**

*Hook:* "How do you know if you need to file a U.S. tax return? There's a simple number."

For Tax Year 2025, if you're single and under 65, the number is $14,600.

That's the standard deduction — and it's your filing threshold.

If your gross income is below $14,600, you are not required to file.

But — if your employer withheld taxes from your paycheck? You should file anyway. It's the only way to get that money back as a refund.

Full threshold table for all filing statuses at AskLinTax.com.

---

**Short #2: "File Even If You Don't Have To"**

*Hook:* "Not required to file a tax return? You might still want to."

Four reasons to file even when you're not legally required:

One — taxes were withheld from your paycheck. File to get the refund.

Two — you qualify for refundable credits like the EITC or Child Tax Credit. File to claim them.

Three — you need a filing history for your visa, mortgage, or green card.

Four — you want to contribute to an IRA.

"Not required" doesn't mean "shouldn't."

---

**Short #3: "F-1 Students: You Still Have to File Something"**

*Hook:* "F-1 student with no U.S. income? You still have a tax obligation."

If you're on an F-1 visa and had no U.S. income, you don't file a regular tax return.

But you must file Form 8843 every year.

Form 8843 documents your exempt individual status — it tells the IRS you're an F-1 student and your days in the U.S. don't count toward the Substantial Presence Test.

It's not a tax form. It's a disclosure form. But failing to file it can cause problems later.
