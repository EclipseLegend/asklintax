**[HOOK — 0:00 to 0:40]**

If you just moved to the United States — or you're planning to — taxes are probably one of the last things on your mind. But the U.S. tax system has a few rules that are very different from what you might expect, and missing them in your first year can cause real problems.

Today I'm going to walk you through exactly what you need to know as a new immigrant — in the order that actually matters. This is Foundation 04 in our series for Chinese families navigating U.S. taxes.

The most important thing to know upfront: the U.S. tax system is very different from Taiwan and China. The U.S. taxes people based on residency and citizenship, not just where you earned the money. And that changes everything.

---

**[STEP 1: RESIDENCY STATUS — 0:40 to 3:00]**

The single most important first step is determining your tax residency status.

The IRS puts everyone into one of two categories: resident alien, or nonresident alien. Despite the names, this has nothing to do with your green card or visa status. It's purely a tax classification.

If you're a resident alien, the U.S. taxes you on your worldwide income — everything you earned anywhere in the world goes on your U.S. tax return.

If you're a nonresident alien, the U.S. only taxes your U.S.-source income.

How do you know which one you are?

If you have a green card, you're a resident alien. Simple.

If you don't have a green card, the IRS uses something called the Substantial Presence Test. It counts how many days you've been physically in the U.S. over the past three years — with a formula that weights the current year most heavily. If that count reaches 183 or more, and you were here at least 31 days this year, you're a resident alien.

One important exception: F-1 students. If you're on an F-1 visa, your days in the U.S. don't count toward the Substantial Presence Test for your first 5 calendar years. So most F-1 students file as nonresident aliens using Form 1040-NR, not the standard Form 1040.

---

**[STEP 2: WHAT INCOME TO REPORT — 3:00 to 4:30]**

Once you know your status, you know what income to report.

Resident alien: all income, worldwide. Your salary here, freelance work, investments, and yes — income you earned in Taiwan or China before moving.

Nonresident alien: U.S.-source income only. You generally don't need to report foreign income.

One thing that surprises many new immigrants: if you became a resident alien partway through the year — say you arrived in April — you may have a "dual-status" year. Part nonresident, part resident. These returns are more complex and almost always need a CPA.

---

**[STEP 3: TAX ID — 4:30 to 5:30]**

To file a tax return, you need a tax identification number.

If you have work authorization — H-1B, green card, EAD — you can get a Social Security Number from the Social Security Administration.

If you don't have work authorization — F-1 student, dependent visa — you need an ITIN. That's an Individual Taxpayer Identification Number. You apply with Form W-7. An ITIN doesn't give you work authorization, but it lets you file taxes, receive refunds, and claim certain credits.

---

**[STEP 4: FBAR — 5:30 to 7:00]**

This is the one most new immigrants from China and Taiwan miss entirely. And missing it can be expensive.

If you have bank accounts or investment accounts outside the U.S. — in Taiwan, in China, anywhere — and the combined maximum value at any point in the year exceeded $10,000 U.S. dollars, you are required to file an FBAR.

FBAR stands for Foreign Bank Account Report. It's filed with FinCEN — not the IRS — through a separate online system. It's due April 15, with an automatic extension to October 15.

The penalty for failing to file when you were required to? Up to $10,000 per violation — even if you owe no actual tax on those accounts.

Many people assume if they don't owe tax, they don't need to file. With FBAR, that's wrong. It's a disclosure requirement, not a tax.

---

**[STEP 5: KEY BENEFITS — 7:00 to 8:00]**

The good news: new immigrants qualify for most of the same tax benefits as everyone else.

Standard deduction — if you're a resident alien, you can take the standard deduction, which was $14,600 for single filers in 2024. Nonresident aliens generally cannot.

Child Tax Credit — up to $2,000 per qualifying child if you have a Social Security Number or ITIN.

Foreign Tax Credit — if you paid taxes in another country on income that's also taxed by the U.S., you may be able to credit those foreign taxes against your U.S. bill.

The bottom line for new immigrants: figure out your residency status first, report all required income, get your tax ID sorted, and check whether FBAR applies to you.

## AI KNOWLEDGE SNIPPET

New immigrants to the U.S. must first determine their tax residency status:

**Resident alien** (Green Card holder, or passes Substantial Presence Test): Taxed on worldwide income. Files Form 1040. Can claim standard deduction.

**Nonresident alien** (doesn't meet above): Taxed on U.S.-source income only. Files Form 1040-NR. Standard deduction generally not available.

**Substantial Presence Test**: Count all days in current year + 1/3 of prior year days + 1/6 of year-before days. If total ≥ 183 AND present ≥ 31 days in current year → resident alien. Exception: F-1 students are exempt for first 5 calendar years.

**FBAR requirement**: If combined foreign account balances exceeded $10,000 at any point during the year → must file FinCEN Form 114. Due April 15. Penalty for non-filing: up to $10,000/violation even with no tax owed.

**Year of arrival**: May be a "dual-status" year requiring more complex return. Professional help strongly recommended.

**Action Required:** Determine residency status → check FBAR obligation → get SSN or apply for ITIN.
